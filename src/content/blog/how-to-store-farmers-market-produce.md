One of the most common complaints about shopping at the farmers market is that the produce goes bad faster than grocery store food. This is actually a sign of quality — local produce hasn't been treated with wax coatings, preservative gases, or weeks of cold storage to extend shelf life. It's fresher, which means it's more alive, which means it needs a little more attention once you get it home.

Here's how to store the most common summer market finds so you get the most out of every purchase.

## Strawberries

Don't wash them until right before eating — moisture speeds up mold. Store in the refrigerator loosely in a single layer or in a container with airflow, not sealed tight. Plan to use within 1–2 days of purchase. If you bought too many, hull them and freeze flat on a sheet pan, then transfer to a zip bag. Frozen local strawberries are far better than anything you'll buy frozen from a store.

## Leafy Greens (Kale, Chard, Lettuce, Spinach, Arugula)

Wrap loosely in a damp paper towel and store in a plastic bag or container in the refrigerator. Kale and chard keep well for 5–7 days this way. Lettuce and spinach are more delicate — aim to use within 3–4 days. Don't wash until ready to use.

## Fresh Herbs

Treat them like flowers. Trim the stems, put them in a glass with an inch of water, and store on the counter (basil) or loosely covered in the refrigerator (parsley, cilantro, dill, mint). Basil hates cold — keep it at room temperature out of direct sunlight. Most herbs stored this way will last 7–10 days.

## Radishes and Turnips

Remove the greens (they draw moisture from the root) and store the roots in a bag in the refrigerator. They'll keep for 1–2 weeks. The greens are edible — sauté them like spinach within a day or two.

## Scallions and Green Onions

Store upright in a glass with a little water, like herbs, or wrap in a damp paper towel in the refrigerator. They last 1–2 weeks.

## Peas (Shelling and Snap)

Refrigerate unwashed in a bag or container. Fresh peas are best used within 2–3 days — the sugars convert to starch quickly after picking. If you have too many, blanch them in boiling water for 1–2 minutes, cool in ice water, and freeze.

## Zucchini and Summer Squash

Store unwashed in the refrigerator crisper drawer. They keep well for about a week. Don't store near apples or bananas — ethylene gas from those fruits speeds up ripening in vegetables.

## Tomatoes

Never refrigerate tomatoes — cold temperatures destroy their flavor and texture. Store at room temperature on the counter, out of direct sunlight, stem side down. Use within a few days of purchase. If they start getting very soft, roast or sauce them rather than eating raw.

## Cucumbers

Cucumbers are sensitive to cold — store at room temperature if you plan to use them within a day or two, or in the warmest part of the refrigerator (the door). They go limp quickly when too cold. Use within 3–5 days.

## The General Rules

- **Don't wash until ready to use.** Moisture speeds up decay.
- **Separate ethylene producers.** Apples, pears, and tomatoes give off gas that ripens nearby vegetables faster.
- **Use the most delicate things first.** Lettuce and fresh herbs before kale and root vegetables.
- **When in doubt, freeze it.** Most vegetables can be blanched and frozen if you realize you won't get to them in time.
